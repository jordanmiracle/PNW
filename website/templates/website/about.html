Stump Removal

Once a tree is removed, the stump can remain and it will continue to grow, as will the roots. Stumps, in and of themselves, are not harmful. However, they can often get in the way of planting new trees, gardens, or obstruct construction projects. Eventually, it is also possible for the roots to begin to push up foundations or fencing. And, of course, stumps can quickly become a home for termites. Which is fine while out in the forest, not near your home!

This is where we come in. After removing a tree, our team can remove the stump in the most effective way possible. Removing the stump of an old tree, in particular, can be incredibly time-consuming and challenging without the proper knowledge and equipment. We are able to grind down stumps in a quick and efficient manner.



Trimming & Pruning

Trimming vs. Pruning

While these terms are often used interchangeably, there is actually a specific functional difference between the two.

Pruning is generally associated with trees and shrubs whose health would be greatly improved by the removal of dead, diseased, or broken branches. Proper pruning, done by certified professionals, removes these potential hazards.

Trimming, on the other hand is generally applied to shrubs, hedges, and trees for design purposes. Apart from offering a more pleasing aesthetic, trimming can also remove excessively dense growth, and allow for sunlight and moisture to reach other areas.




Windsailing and Crown Reduction

Crown-reduction is basically reducing the overall size of a tree’s canopy by using reduction cuts on many of the secondary (smaller) branches all around the outside — shortening as many of the limbs as possible. The best application of crown-reduction is when a tree has been planted in a small area. It isn’t uncommon for a sapling tree to be planted with little to no consideration given to how large it may eventually become — which varies depending on the species as well as environmental factors (such as watering and soil quality).

Mindful planning of the potential sizes of various tree in a yard is tricky, and if mistakes have been made, crown-reduction can help with overcrowding in the yard. Some trees are kept small purely for artistic reasons as well. Bonsai trees are classic examples of crown-reduction in the extreme, where the growth, the size of a tree, is severely scaled back.

Cabling & Deadwooding

Cabling

Similar to a human with bad posture or spinal problems, a tree can start to grow in a manner that is harmful when it is still relatively young. If this kind of harmful growth is allowed to continue unabated, the tree will eventually be unable to support itself. This can lead to obvious problems, such as the tree damaging your home, or simply needing to be removed altogether. This is where our team’s training with cabling techniques comes in. When cabling is professionally applied, it can correct a tree’s growth and allow it to redirect in a safe manner.


Deadwooding

Deadwooding is, in practice, basically what it sounds like. This is the practice of trimming dead wood from a tree. Over time, branches large and small start to die and fall from a tree. This can happen due to a variety of factors. One scenario that is common in urban or suburban areas is a tree that only receives sunlight on one side, leading to the tree not getting enough nutrients on the other side. Deadwooding removes these parts of the tree before any major issues occur. This keeps the tree healthier, and reduces the liklihood of a large branch falling and causing damage.
